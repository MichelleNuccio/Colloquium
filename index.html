01

Research question

Contemporary interfaces allow us to navigate the Internet while simultaneously preventing us from perceiving it as a territory.

Browsers, cloud services, applications, and AI systems privilege accessibility while concealing the computational, infrastructural, and relational processes through which digital space is continuously produced.

Khôra begins from a different premise. Rather than asking how the Internet can be visualised or mapped, the project asks whether it can be understood as a territory.

Central inquiry Can the Internet be understood as a dynamic territory with its own geomorphology, and what forms of representation can observe it without reducing it to a static map?

Rather than producing another representation of the Internet, the project investigates how representation itself must evolve when its object is a continuously transforming computational territory.

Situated technology

The Internet is not an abstract or immaterial cloud.

Physical infrastructuresComputational systemsEcological resourcesProtocolsAlgorithmsHuman activity

These situated relationships continuously generate a spatial condition with its own evolving morphology: a territory whose form is constantly negotiated rather than permanently established.

06

Representation as inquiry

Drawing does not illustrate existing knowledge. It becomes the means through which computational territory is investigated.

Like Land Art, Khôra does not invent landscapes; it establishes new ways of observing and engaging with territories that already exist. The challenge is not invention, but observation.

Every drawing records only a temporary condition. Successive observations accumulate, allowing the computational landscape gradually to emerge.

Terrain precedes representation.

Landscape emerges through representation.
