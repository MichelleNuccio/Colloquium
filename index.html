01

Introduction

The Internet has become increasingly accessible while simultaneously becoming increasingly difficult to perceive.

Interfaces compress vast computational processes into seamless interactions, allowing users to navigate digital environments without confronting the infrastructures, protocols, algorithms, and relations that sustain them.

This simplification is a defining achievement of interface design. Yet contemporary interfaces may not simply conceal technological complexity; they may also reduce our ability to perceive the Internet as a spatial condition.

Initial inquiryIf interfaces continuously abstract computation, what is left outside the interface?

Rather than asking what the interface reveals, Khôra asks what it leaves behind.

02

The Interface

Every interface performs an act of selection.

Only the elements necessary for interaction remain visible; everything else is abstracted from experience. This process may also remove the spatial relationships through which computation exists.

Protocols, servers, algorithms, energy systems, data centres, and information flows continue to operate, yet they are no longer experienced as components of a shared spatial condition.

The Internet becomes something we use

rather than somewhere we inhabit.

03

The Research Problem

Existing research has expanded our understanding of digital technologies. Yet it often remains concerned with explaining systems.

Researchers have revealed global infrastructures, exposed supply chains, analysed data flows, mapped networks, and described planetary computation.

Khôra asks whether interfaces have also transformed how the Internet can be perceived spatially—and what forms of inquiry might make computational space observable once again.

04

Research Question

The Internet may be approached not only as a network or infrastructure, but also as a territorial condition.

Central inquiryCan the Internet be understood as a dynamic territory, and if so, what forms of representation might allow its continuously evolving spatial condition to be observed?

The research investigates whether representation itself can function as a method of inquiry into computational space.

05

Conceptual Position

A shift from network thinking towards territorial thinking.

Begin with

Processes

Environments whose form emerges through continuous transformation.

Investigate

Morphologies

The formation, evolution, and interaction of dynamic spatial systems.

Geomorphology is not a visual metaphor. It is a conceptual framework for approaching computational environments through processes rather than maps.

06

Why Khôra?

χώρα khōra — land, region, territory.

In Plato's Timaeus, khôra describes a receptive condition through which forms may emerge without itself possessing a stable form.

The term is not a philosophical foundation or a claim that the Internet is a khôra. It evokes spatial conditions that resist fixed representation and whose morphology must be investigated.

OrientationKhôra names not the object of the research, but the space of inquiry itself.

08

Representation as Observation

Drawing becomes an instrument for producing new knowledge.

Like Land Art, Khôra does not invent landscapes. It establishes new ways of perceiving territories that already exist.

Each drawing records only a temporary condition. Successive representations progressively construct an understanding of computational territory.

Landscape is not assumed.

It emerges through observation.

09

Methodology

Theoretical inquiry meets computational design through five interconnected stages.

  1. 01

    Observation

    Identify phenomena that may describe territorial conditions.

  2. 02

    Collection

    Gather relational datasets across infrastructures, protocols, computation, resources, and flows.

  3. 03

    Translation

    Interpret computational relationships as spatial conditions rather than networks.

  4. 04

    Computation

    Develop procedural models generating dynamic spatial organisations.

  5. 05

    Representation

    Produce drawings that investigate morphology rather than simply visualise data.

11

Expected Contribution

Khôra does not seek to produce another map of the Internet.

Nor does it attempt to reveal hidden infrastructures already documented elsewhere. Its contribution lies in opening a new research direction.

The project establishes a conceptual and methodological framework for asking whether territorial thinking and epistemic representation can make computational spatial conditions observable.
