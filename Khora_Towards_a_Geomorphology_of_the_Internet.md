# Khôra

## *Towards a Geomorphology of the Internet*

> *A research proposal exploring the possibility of understanding the Internet as a territory through representation.*

---

# Introduction

The Internet has become increasingly accessible while simultaneously becoming increasingly difficult to perceive.

Every interaction with contemporary digital systems is mediated through interfaces designed to simplify complexity. Browsers, cloud services, mobile applications, and AI systems compress vast computational processes into seamless interactions, allowing users to navigate digital environments without confronting the infrastructures, protocols, algorithms, and relations that sustain them.

This simplification is one of the defining achievements of interface design.

Yet it also raises an important question.

If interfaces continuously abstract computation, **what is left outside the interface?**

This research begins from the hypothesis that contemporary interfaces may not simply conceal technological complexity, but also reduce our ability to perceive the Internet as a spatial condition.

Rather than asking what the interface reveals, **Khôra** asks what it leaves behind.

---

# The Interface

Interfaces are generally understood as technologies of accessibility.

They reduce complexity, organise information, and facilitate interaction.

To achieve this, every interface performs an act of selection.

Only the elements necessary for interaction remain visible; everything else is abstracted from experience.

This process does not simply hide technical infrastructures.

It may also remove the spatial relationships through which computation exists.

Protocols, servers, algorithms, energy systems, data centres, and information flows continue to operate, yet they are no longer experienced as components of a shared spatial condition.

The Internet becomes something we use rather than somewhere we inhabit.

This observation does not imply that territory disappears materially.

Rather, it suggests that the **perception of territoriality** is progressively displaced by the logic of the interface.

---

# The Research Problem

Much of the existing literature has successfully investigated what lies behind contemporary interfaces.

Researchers have revealed global infrastructures, exposed supply chains, analysed data flows, mapped networks, and described planetary computation.

These contributions have profoundly expanded our understanding of digital technologies.

Yet they often remain concerned with explaining systems.

This research asks a different question.

Rather than investigating what the Internet is made of, it asks whether contemporary interfaces have also transformed **how the Internet can be perceived spatially**.

If interfaces privilege interaction over spatial understanding, what forms of inquiry might allow computational space to become observable once again?

---

# Research Question

This project explores the possibility that the Internet may be approached not only as a network or infrastructure, but also as a territorial condition.

It therefore asks:

> **Can the Internet be understood as a dynamic territory, and if so, what forms of representation might allow its continuously evolving spatial condition to be observed?**

Rather than seeking a definitive representation of the Internet, the research investigates whether representation itself can function as a method of inquiry into computational space.

---

# Conceptual Position

Khôra proposes a shift from **network thinking** towards **territorial thinking**.

This shift is not intended to replace existing understandings of digital infrastructures, but to complement them through a different spatial perspective.

Within this research, geomorphology is not employed as a visual metaphor.

Instead, it is considered a conceptual framework for studying environments whose form emerges through continuous processes of transformation.

Where traditional cartography often describes the location of stable objects, geomorphological thinking investigates the formation, evolution, and interaction of dynamic spatial systems.

The research therefore explores whether similar modes of thinking may contribute to understanding computational environments.

Rather than beginning with maps, it begins with processes.

Rather than describing objects, it investigates morphologies.

---

# Why Khôra?

The title **Khôra** derives from the ancient Greek **χώρα** (*khōra*), a term commonly translated as *land*, *region*, or *territory*.

Beyond its geographical meaning, Plato's *Timaeus* uses **khôra** to describe a receptive condition through which forms may emerge without itself possessing a stable form.

This research does not adopt Plato's concept as a philosophical foundation, nor does it suggest that the Internet *is* a khôra.

Instead, the term functions as a conceptual orientation.

It evokes the possibility of approaching computational environments as spatial conditions that resist fixed representation and whose morphology must be investigated rather than assumed.

In this sense, **Khôra names not the object of the research, but the space of inquiry itself.**

---

# Community of Practice

Khôra is situated at the intersection of architecture, computational design, representation studies, media theory, and digital geography.

Its contribution develops through dialogue with existing research while proposing a distinct representational trajectory.

James Corner demonstrated that mapping actively constructs knowledge rather than merely documenting reality.

*Terra Forma* proposed alternative representational methods capable of describing ecological relationships beyond conventional cartography.

Keller Easterling redefined infrastructure as an active spatial organisation rather than hidden technical support.

Benjamin Bratton framed planetary computation as a new geopolitical and spatial condition.

Kate Crawford and Vladan Joler demonstrated that drawing itself can operate as a critical research methodology through projects such as *Calculating Empires*.

Khôra builds upon these contributions while asking a different question:

Can computational systems be investigated as territories whose morphology continuously evolves?

---

# Representation as Observation

Representation constitutes the primary methodology of the research.

Rather than illustrating previously established knowledge, drawing becomes an instrument for producing new knowledge.

In this respect, the project resonates methodologically with Land Art.

Land Art does not invent landscapes.

It establishes new ways of perceiving territories that already exist.

Similarly, Khôra does not propose to design a new digital environment.

It begins from the possibility that the Internet already possesses territorial qualities.

Representation therefore becomes an act of observation.

Each drawing records only a temporary condition of a continuously evolving environment.

Rather than producing a definitive image, successive representations progressively construct an understanding of computational territory.

Landscape is therefore not assumed.

It emerges through observation.

---

# Methodology

The research combines theoretical inquiry with computational design.

Its methodology develops iteratively through five interconnected stages.

**Observation**

Identifying computational phenomena that may describe territorial conditions.

**Collection**

Gathering relational datasets describing infrastructures, protocols, computation, environmental resources, and information flows.

**Translation**

Interpreting computational relationships as spatial conditions rather than abstract networks.

**Computation**

Developing procedural models capable of generating dynamic spatial organisations.

**Representation**

Producing drawings that investigate morphology rather than simply visualising data.

Each iteration informs subsequent experiments, allowing both the representational language and the theoretical framework to evolve together.

---

# Computational Experiments

Rather than pursuing a single final representation, the research develops through successive design experiments.

Each experiment tests a different representational hypothesis.

* **Beyond the Interface** — investigating computational relationships abstracted by interfaces.
* **Relational Territories** — exploring computational fields beyond conventional network diagrams.
* **Geomorphological Translation** — examining how computational relations may generate spatial morphologies.
* **Dynamic Terrain** — developing procedural representations capable of evolving through changing datasets.
* **Living Atlas** — assembling successive observations into an evolving archive of computational territory.

These experiments function not as illustrations of theory but as mechanisms for producing theory.

---

# Expected Contribution

Khôra does not seek to produce another map of the Internet.

Nor does it attempt to reveal hidden infrastructures already documented elsewhere.

Its contribution lies in opening a new research direction.

The project investigates whether computational environments can be understood through territorial thinking, and whether representation can operate as an epistemic practice capable of observing spatial conditions that remain largely absent from contemporary digital experience.

Rather than offering definitive answers, Khôra establishes a conceptual and methodological framework through which these questions can be explored.

---

# Future Development

As an initial research proposal, Khôra defines a territory of investigation rather than a completed theoretical position.

Its immediate objective is to develop a shared vocabulary connecting architecture, computational design, geomorphology, and media theory.

Through iterative computational experiments, the project will progressively refine its theoretical framework, its representational language, and its understanding of the Internet as a possible territorial condition.

The long-term ambition is the development of a **Living Atlas of the Internet**: not a definitive map, but an evolving collection of observations through which computational territory gradually becomes legible.

---

## Working Hypothesis

The project is guided by a simple working hypothesis:

> **Contemporary interfaces may have reduced our ability to perceive the Internet as a spatial condition. Rather than recovering hidden infrastructures, this research investigates whether representation can recover the possibility of thinking computational space as territory.**
