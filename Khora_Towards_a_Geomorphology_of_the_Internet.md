# Khôra
## Towards a Geomorphology of the Internet

---

# Research Question

> *Contemporary interfaces allow us to navigate the Internet while simultaneously preventing us from perceiving it as a territory.*

The Internet is increasingly experienced through interfaces that privilege accessibility while concealing the computational, infrastructural, and relational processes through which digital space is continuously produced. Browsers, cloud services, applications, and AI systems abstract away the complexity of computation, making the Internet appear immaterial, instantaneous, and placeless.

Over the past decades, scholars have revealed the material infrastructures behind digital technologies, mapped network topologies, and analysed the political economies of computation. Yet these approaches continue to describe the Internet primarily as infrastructure, network, or system.

**Khôra begins from a different premise.**

Rather than asking how the Internet can be visualised or mapped, the project asks whether it can be understood as a territory.

Unlike geographical territories, however, the Internet cannot be described through conventional cartographic methods. It is continuously reshaped by protocols, infrastructures, algorithms, data flows, and human activity operating across multiple temporal and spatial scales.

The research therefore asks:

> **Can the Internet be understood as a dynamic territory with its own geomorphology, and what forms of representation are capable of observing such a territory without reducing it to a static map?**

Rather than producing another representation of the Internet, the project investigates how representation itself must evolve when its object is a continuously transforming computational territory.

---

# Conceptual Position

Khôra proposes a shift from **network thinking** to **territorial thinking**.

The project argues that the Internet should no longer be understood exclusively as infrastructure or connectivity, but as an evolving spatial condition whose morphology emerges through the interaction of physical infrastructures, computational processes, protocols, environmental resources, and social practices.

In this context, **geomorphology** is not adopted as a visual metaphor.

Instead, it functions as an epistemological framework.

Where cartography traditionally asks *where things are*, geomorphology asks *how territories acquire form, transform over time, and generate spatial organization*.

This shift allows the Internet to be approached not as an invisible system requiring visualisation, but as a territory requiring observation.

# Community of Practice

Khôra is situated at the intersection of architecture, computational design, representation studies, media theory, and digital geography.

Its contribution emerges through dialogue with existing research while proposing a different representational trajectory.

## James Corner

Corner demonstrates that mapping is not simply descriptive but an active process of constructing knowledge. Khôra extends this position by questioning whether conventional mapping remains sufficient for computational territories.

## Terra Forma

Terra Forma proposes alternative modes of representing terrestrial landscapes through ecological and relational thinking. Khôra extends this representational methodology beyond physical geography towards computational environments.

## Keller Easterling

Easterling reveals infrastructure as an active spatial organisation rather than hidden technical support. Khôra builds upon this perspective while shifting attention from infrastructure itself towards the territory emerging through infrastructural relations.

## Benjamin Bratton

Bratton's conception of planetary-scale computation provides a critical framework for understanding digital infrastructures. Khôra complements this work by investigating how computational systems can also be understood through spatial representation.

## Kate Crawford & Vladan Joler

Projects such as *Calculating Empires* demonstrate that drawing can function as a critical research methodology capable of producing knowledge rather than illustrating it. Khôra adopts this methodological position while redirecting its focus toward the morphology of computational territory.

---

# Relationship to Precedent

**Calculating Empires** represents the project's principal methodological precedent.

Rather than treating drawing as representation after analysis, Crawford and Joler employ representation as analysis itself.

Khôra adopts this epistemological role of drawing while asking a fundamentally different question.

Where *Calculating Empires* investigates the historical and political organisation of computation, Khôra investigates whether these same relationships collectively generate a dynamic territory.

The project therefore constitutes both a reinterpretation and an extension of this representational methodology.

---

# Situated Technology

Khôra rejects the notion of the Internet as an abstract or immaterial cloud.

Instead, it understands the Internet as a situated territory emerging through physical infrastructures, computational systems, ecological resources, protocols, algorithms, and human activity.

The project does not simply seek to reveal the materiality of digital technologies.

Its argument is that these situated relationships continuously generate a spatial condition with its own evolving morphology.

The Internet is therefore understood as a territory whose form is constantly negotiated rather than permanently established.

---

# Representation as Inquiry

Representation constitutes the primary research methodology of Khôra.

Rather than illustrating existing knowledge, drawing becomes the means through which computational territory can be investigated.

The project shares an affinity with **Land Art**.

Land Art does not invent landscapes; it establishes new ways of observing and engaging with territories that already exist.

Similarly, Khôra does not seek to design a new digital environment.

It assumes that the Internet already exists as a territory.

The challenge is therefore not one of invention, but of observation.

Every drawing records only a temporary condition of an evolving territory.

Rather than producing a definitive representation, the project accumulates successive observations through which computational landscape gradually emerges.

In this sense:

**Terrain precedes representation.**

**Landscape emerges through representation.**

# Methods

The project combines qualitative theoretical inquiry with computational design methodologies.

Its research develops through an iterative workflow composed of five interconnected stages.

## Observation

Identifying computational phenomena capable of describing specific territorial conditions of the Internet.

## Data Collection

Collecting relational datasets describing interactions between infrastructures, protocols, computational processes, and information flows.

## Translation

Interpreting computational relationships as spatial conditions rather than abstract networks.

## Computation

Developing procedural workflows capable of generating multidimensional spatial organisations through computational design.

## Representation

Producing experimental drawings that investigate morphology rather than visualise information.

Each iteration informs the next, allowing the representational language to evolve alongside the research itself.

Potential tools include Python, Houdini, Blender Geometry Nodes, Grasshopper, and Adobe Illustrator.

---

# Computational Design Experiments

Rather than producing a single final representation, the research develops through successive computational experiments.

Each experiment tests a different representational hypothesis.

**Experiment 01 — Beyond the Interface**

Investigating the computational relationships concealed behind contemporary interfaces.

**Experiment 02 — Relational Territories**

Transforming relational datasets into spatial fields rather than conventional network diagrams.

**Experiment 03 — Geomorphological Translation**

Exploring how computational relations generate conditions analogous to density, stratification, erosion, sedimentation, and gradients.

**Experiment 04 — Dynamic Terrain**

Developing procedural representations capable of evolving through changing datasets.

**Experiment 05 — Living Atlas**

Accumulating successive observations into an evolving atlas documenting the morphology of computational territory.

# Visual Representation

Khôra investigates a visual language positioned between scientific observation, geomorphological survey, speculative cartography, and computational simulation.

Its representational vocabulary draws inspiration from:

- geomorphological surveys
- topographic drawing
- scientific atlases
- procedural modelling
- computational simulation

The project deliberately avoids conventional network visualisations.

Instead, representation seeks to describe the Internet as a layered, multidimensional, and continuously evolving terrain.

Its aesthetic is analytical rather than illustrative.

Drawing functions as an epistemic instrument through which computational territory progressively becomes observable.

---

# Rhetorical Argument

Digital technologies have become increasingly accessible while simultaneously concealing the spatial complexity of the Internet behind simplified interfaces.

Khôra argues that the Internet should no longer be understood solely as infrastructure, network, or cloud.

Instead, it proposes understanding the Internet as a dynamic territory whose morphology continuously emerges through computational, material, ecological, and social relations.

If the Internet constitutes a new form of territory, then existing representational techniques become insufficient.

The project therefore argues that understanding computational space requires the development of new representational methodologies capable of describing transformation rather than static organisation.

---

# Potential Capstone Project

The capstone project proposes the development of a **Living Atlas of the Internet**.

Rather than producing a definitive map, the atlas will accumulate successive observations of computational territory through computational drawings, procedural simulations, physical artefacts, and an evolving digital platform.

Each representation constitutes a temporary observation of an environment that continuously transforms.

Together these observations construct a methodology for investigating the geomorphology of computational space.

# Challenges

Several challenges define the development of this research.

## Defining the Territory

Identifying meaningful computational phenomena capable of describing the Internet as territory rather than infrastructure alone.

## Computational Methodology

Developing procedural workflows capable of translating multidimensional computational relationships into coherent spatial representations.

## Representation

Constructing a genuinely new representational language that moves beyond conventional data visualisation while remaining analytically rigorous.

## Theory

Building a robust dialogue between architecture, geomorphology, computational design, media theory, and Internet studies without relying on purely metaphorical analogies.

## Scale

Understanding how local computational events relate to planetary infrastructures and how multiple spatial scales may coexist within the same representational framework.
