# Khôra
### Towards a Geomorphology of the Internet

---

# Research Question

Contemporary digital interfaces have made the Internet increasingly accessible while simultaneously obscuring its spatial complexity. While users interact through browsers, applications, and AI interfaces, the computational, infrastructural, and relational processes that sustain these interactions remain largely invisible.

Existing research has predominantly focused on making digital infrastructures visible through network mapping, critical cartography, and infrastructural analysis. **Khôra** shifts this perspective by asking whether the Internet can be understood as a dynamic territory with its own geomorphology.

Rather than asking *how the Internet can be mapped*, the project investigates:

> **Can the Internet be understood as a dynamic territory with its own geomorphology, and if so, how can such a territory be represented without reducing it to a static map?**

The research proposes that representing the Internet requires a new spatial language capable of describing continuous transformation rather than fixed topology.

---

# Community of Practice

The project positions itself at the intersection of architecture, computational design, media theory, critical cartography, and digital geography.

Its research builds upon several important precedents while shifting their focus toward representation as a spatial investigation.

## James Corner

Corner's work on agency and critical cartography demonstrates that maps are not neutral descriptions but instruments for constructing knowledge. Khôra extends this understanding by questioning whether mapping alone is sufficient to represent computational territories.

## Terra Forma

Terra Forma proposes alternative methods for representing terrestrial landscapes through relational and ecological thinking. Khôra extends this methodology from physical landscapes to computational space, investigating how similar representational strategies might operate within the Internet.

## Keller Easterling

Easterling's work reveals infrastructure as an active spatial organization rather than a hidden technical system. Khôra builds upon this perspective but focuses on the territory emerging from these infrastructures rather than the infrastructures themselves.

## Benjamin Bratton

Bratton's planetary-scale computation provides an important theoretical framework for understanding digital infrastructures. Khôra shifts attention from governance and political organization toward spatial representation.

## Kate Crawford & Vladan Joler

Projects such as *Calculating Empires* demonstrate that drawing can become a powerful instrument for investigating computational systems. Khôra adopts representation as a research method while shifting the question from historical and political genealogy toward the spatial morphology of the Internet.

---

# Relationship to Precedent

**Calculating Empires** functions as an important methodological precedent.

Rather than treating drawing as illustration, Crawford and Joler use representation to construct knowledge about computation and power.

Khôra adopts this epistemological role of representation while proposing a different research question.

Where *Calculating Empires* investigates how computation has shaped political, economic, and technological systems, Khôra investigates whether these relationships collectively produce a dynamic territory with its own morphology.

The project can therefore be understood as a reinterpretation and extension of this representational methodology.

---

# Situated Technology

Khôra rejects the notion of the Internet as an abstract or immaterial space.

Instead, it understands the Internet as a situated territory emerging through physical infrastructures, computational systems, protocols, data flows, ecological resources, and human activity.

Its spatial condition is continuously produced through relationships operating across multiple scales.

Rather than simply demonstrating the materiality of digital technologies, the project investigates how these relationships generate an evolving computational terrain requiring new forms of representation.

---

# Methods

The research combines qualitative and computational methods.

Rather than producing conventional data visualizations, the project develops computational workflows capable of translating relational datasets into experimental spatial representations.

The methodology consists of four iterative stages:

1. Observation of computational phenomena through selected datasets.
2. Interpretation of relational structures as spatial conditions.
3. Procedural generation of multidimensional representations.
4. Critical drawing as a form of spatial investigation.

Potential computational tools include:

- Python for data collection and processing.
- Houdini for procedural modelling and simulation.
- Blender Geometry Nodes and Grasshopper for spatial experimentation.
- Adobe Illustrator for representational refinement.

The project is expected to combine qualitative theoretical analysis with quantitative computational processes.

Outputs may include computational drawings, procedural simulations, physical models, printed atlases, and an evolving digital platform documenting the research process.
